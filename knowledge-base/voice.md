# CA Global Group — Brand Voice Guide

*For use in AI content generation, copywriting, and communications training*

---

## Overview

CA Global Group encompasses three specialist executive recruitment brands — **CA Global Headhunters**, **CA Mining**, and **CA Global Finance** — each serving distinct sectors but united by a shared identity: deep African market expertise, relationship-driven practice, and senior-level recruitment credibility. This guide defines the voice standards that apply across all three brands, with noted variations where sector context demands it.

---

## 1. Overall Tone and Register

### Primary Tone: Authoritative yet Approachable

The CA Global Group occupies a senior professional space — executive search, headhunting, and C-suite placements — but does not communicate with cold corporate distance. The tone is **confident without being arrogant**, **expert without being exclusionary**, and **warm without being casual**.

Think: a highly experienced executive recruiter speaking across the table — someone who knows Africa's markets intimately, respects both their client's time and their candidate's career, and earns trust through knowledge rather than jargon.

### Register Levels by Context

| Context | Register |
|---|---|
| Website copy (About, Services) | Formal-professional, measured, confident |
| Blog content | Semi-formal, informative, conversational |
| Candidate-facing comms | Warm, direct, encouraging, respectful |
| Client-facing comms | Authoritative, consultative, results-oriented |
| Social media | Accessible, energetic, still professional |

### Core Tone Attributes

- **Specialist confidence** — We speak with the authority of lived experience in African markets, not borrowed credibility
- **Relationship-first** — Language reflects long-term partnerships, not transactional exchanges
- **Continent-proud** — Africa is framed as a landscape of opportunity and complexity, never as a difficult afterthought
- **Quietly ambitious** — Achievements are stated clearly; boasting is avoided

## 2. Writing Style Characteristics

### Sentence Length and Rhythm

- **Preferred sentence length:** Medium — typically 15–25 words for body copy
- **Use short sentences** (6–12 words) for emphasis, calls to action, or opening hooks
- **Avoid very long sentences** (30+ words) that bury the key message
- **Vary rhythm deliberately** — a short punchy sentence after two medium ones creates cadence and keeps readers engaged
- Paragraphs should be **3–5 sentences maximum** in digital copy; single-sentence paragraphs are acceptable for impact

**Example of preferred rhythm:**
> *Hiring at the C-level is not the same as filling a vacancy. It requires a different approach — one built on market intelligence, discretion, and long-standing relationships. That is what we do.*

### Sentence Structure

- **Active voice preferred** at all times — subject acts, results follow
- Passive voice only where the subject is genuinely unknown or where formality demands it
- Avoid nominalisations (turning verbs into nouns): write *"we recruit"* not *"we undertake recruitment"*
- Use **parallel structure** in lists and service descriptions for clarity and professionalism
- Em-dashes (—) and colons are welcome for emphasis and clause separation; semicolons should be used sparingly

### Vocabulary Level

- **C2 English comprehension assumed** for most audiences, but clarity always wins over complexity
- Use **precise, sector-appropriate vocabulary** — do not dumb down, but do not show off
- Favour **Anglo-Saxon root words** over Latinate equivalents where possible: *use* not *utilise*, *help* not *facilitate*, *find* not *identify and source*
- **Avoid buzzwords and filler phrases** that dilute meaning (see Section 7)
- Numbers: write out one through nine; use numerals for 10 and above; always use numerals for statistics (*"over 1,200 placements"*)

### Point of View

- **First person plural (we/our)** for brand-level copy — this reflects the collective team and relationship model
- **Second person (you/your)** for direct candidate or client communications — brings immediacy and personalisation
- Avoid third-person self-reference (*"CA Global believes…"*) except in formal press or award contexts

## 3. Candidate-Facing Communication Style

### Mindset

Candidates — especially at executive level — are not passive applicants. They are high-performing professionals with choices, reputations, and specific career ambitions. The voice must reflect that understanding. We are **partners in their career**, not gatekeepers to a job.

### Tone Specifics

- **Respectful and direct** — assume intelligence; do not over-explain basics
- **Encouraging without being sycophantic** — acknowledge calibre without empty flattery
- **Honest about process** — transparency builds trust at senior level
- **Continent-aware** — acknowledge that moving into or across Africa is a significant career and life decision

### Language Patterns

- Lead with **opportunity and growth**, not just job title and location
- Reference **market context** — why this role, why now, why this market
- Use **second person** naturally: *"Your experience in Francophone West Africa positions you well for…"*
- Acknowledge **diaspora and expatriate candidates** explicitly where relevant — they are a named part of the talent strategy
- Avoid language that implies candidates should be grateful; frame as mutual fit assessment

### Structural Approach for Candidate Content

1. Hook with market insight or career opportunity framing
2. Describe the role or opportunity in context (not just bullet points)
3. Speak to the ideal candidate profile in aspirational terms
4. Articulate what the candidate gains — not just what the employer needs
5. Clear, low-friction call to action

**Example candidate-facing paragraph:**
> *Whether you are building a career across Sub-Saharan Africa or considering your next move into a high-growth market, our consultants work with you — not just for you. We take the time to understand where you want to go, not just where you have been.*

## 4. Client-Facing Communication Style

### Mindset

Clients are organisations with commercial pressures, talent gaps at the highest levels, and reputations to protect. They need a recruitment partner who demonstrates **market knowledge**, **discretion**, and **a track record of delivery** — not a vendor pitching services. The voice must reflect **peer-level consultative expertise**.

### Tone Specifics

- **Confident and results-anchored** — reference outcomes, not just process
- **Consultative, not salesy** — position CA Global as a strategic partner, not a service provider
- **Specific where possible** — vague claims erode credibility at executive level
- **Sector-fluent** — language should reflect literacy in the client's world (mining operations, financial services, oil and gas, etc.)

### Language Patterns

- Lead with the **business problem**, not the service offering
- Use **outcome language**: placements made, time-to-hire reduced, talent gaps closed
- Reference **Africa-specific market dynamics** — this is the core differentiator
- Acknowledge **complexity**: multilingual markets, diaspora talent pools, regulatory environments, remote operations
- Never oversell timelines or make guarantees that cannot be substantiated

### Structural Approach for Client Content

1. Acknowledge the challenge or business context
2. Demonstrate understanding of the sector and African market landscape
3. Articulate the CA Global approach and differentiators
4. Provide evidence (placements, sectors covered, client types)
5. Clear next step or consultation invitation

**Example client-facing paragraph:**
> *Finding the right Chief Financial Officer for a mid-tier mining operation in the DRC requires more than a database search. It requires an understanding of operational realities on the ground, fluency in Francophone market dynamics, and a network built over years — not weeks. That is the depth we bring to every retained search.*

## 5. Use of Industry Terminology

### General Principle

Use industry terminology **purposefully and accurately** — it signals expertise but should never exclude or intimidate. Terms should be used where they add precision, not where they simply sound impressive.

### Approved Terminology by Brand

**CA Global Headhunters (Group-wide)**
- Executive search / retained search / contingency search
- Headhunting / headhunter (used openly and without apology — the brand leans into this term)
- C-suite, C-level executives
- African Diaspora (capitalised when referring to the talent community)
- Recruitment Process Outsourcing (RPO)
- Board-level appointments
- Scarce skills / niche skills
- Lusophone Africa / Francophone Africa (used to signal linguistic capability)

**CA Mining**
- Mineral exploration / mine production / mine maintenance
- EPC / EPCM (Engineering, Procurement and Construction Management)
- Contract mining / service providers
- Discipline-specific recruitment
- Renewable energy (increasingly relevant in portfolio)
- Project development pipeline

**CA Global Finance**
- DFI (Development Finance Institutions)
- Private equity / microfinance / FinTech
- FMCG (in financial and commodities context)
- Financial services sector
- Sub-Saharan and Northern Africa
- Multilingual capability (Portuguese / French markets)

### Terminology to Handle with Care

- **"Headhunter"** — Use confidently in owned channels; may need contextualisation in markets unfamiliar with the term
- **"Executive"** — Do not dilute by applying to mid-level roles; reserve for genuine senior appointments
- **"Africa"** — Never treat as monolithic; specific sub-regions, countries, and markets should be named where possible

## 6. Typical Content Structure Patterns

### Blog Posts

```
1. Headline — specific, benefit-led, Africa-contextualised
2. Opening hook — a problem, a statistic, a market shift (2–3 sentences)
3. Context section — why this matters now in the African market
4. Body — 3–5 structured sections with subheadings
5. Practical takeaways — actionable insights for the reader
6. Closing paragraph — forward-looking, brand-consistent
7. CTA — soft, relationship-oriented ("Speak to our team" vs "Buy now")
```

### Service Pages

```
1. Service name + one-line definition
2. Problem this service solves (client-facing framing)
3. How CA Global approaches this service (differentiators)
4. What the client/candidate can expect (process transparency)
5. Supporting evidence or sector examples
6. CTA — consultation or contact
```

### About / Brand Pages

```
1. Mission or positioning statement (bold, brief)
2. Founding context and credentials (establishes authority)
3. Geographic scope and sector reach
4. Team and relationship philosophy
5. Evidence of outcomes (placements, years, markets)
6. Group structure context where relevant
```

### Job Postings

```
1. Role title + country/region (specific, not vague)
2. Company/client context (industry, size, significance)
3. Role overview — narrative, not just duties
4. Key responsibilities — bulleted, parallel structure
5. Candidate requirements — essential vs advantageous
6. What the role offers — framed for the candidate's benefit
7. Application CTA — clear, low-friction
```

## 7. What to Avoid

### Tone Pitfalls

- ❌ **Arrogance masquerading as authority** — "We are Africa's best recruitment firm" without substantiation
- ❌ **Empty warmth** — "We are passionate about people" without specificity or follow-through
- ❌ **Salesy urgency** — "Don't miss out!", "Act now!", "Limited spots available"
- ❌ **False informality** — Slang, exclamation marks in body copy, emoji in formal communications
- ❌ **Paternalism toward Africa** — Any framing that positions Africa as a challenge to be solved rather than a sophisticated, diverse landscape of markets

### Writing Quality Pitfalls

- ❌ **Clichés:** "hit the ground running", "go the extra mile", "synergy", "leverage", "dynamic team player"
- ❌ **Nominalisations:** "provide assistance" → use "help"; "undertake an assessment" → use "assess"
- ❌ **Passive constructions as defaults:** "Candidates will be contacted" → "We will contact you"
- ❌ **Vague claims without evidence:** "We have an extensive network" → "We have placed executives in 54 African markets across 15 sectors"
- ❌ **Over-use of adjectives:** Let specificity do the work that adjectives try to do

### Brand-Specific Pitfalls

- ❌ Treating all three brands as identical — CA Mining, CA Finance, and CA Global have distinct sector voices
- ❌ Using "Africa" as a single market — always specify regions, languages, or countries where possible
- ❌ Conflating candidate and client audiences in the same piece without clear transitions
- ❌ Describing executive roles with generalist language
- ❌ Overstating guarantees around placement timelines or outcomes

## 8. Example Phrases That Capture the Voice

These phrases demonstrate the balance of authority, specificity, warmth, and African market focus that defines the CA Global Group voice.

---

**① On expertise and market depth:**
> *"We do not simply search — we engage. Our consultants are embedded in the markets they serve, whether that is a Francophone West African banking landscape or an underground mining operation in the DRC."*

---

**② On the candidate relationship:**
> *"Your next move is too important to leave to a database. We take the time to understand your career on its own terms — where you have been, where you want to go, and what Africa's most exciting opportunities look like for you specifically."*

---

**③ On Africa as opportunity (not obstacle):**
> *"Africa's diversity is not a complication — it is the point. Our multilingual, continent-wide network exists precisely because no two markets here are the same, and the best talent often sits at the intersection of local knowledge and international experience."*

---

**④ On the distinction between recruiting and executive search:**
> *"Filling a vacancy and finding the right executive are fundamentally different exercises. One starts with a job description. The other starts with understanding your business, your culture, and the kind of leadership your next chapter demands."*

---

**⑤ On track record without boasting:**
> *"With over 1,200 placements across Africa's financial services sector, we have earned the trust of DFIs, private equity houses, and regional banks alike — not through promises, but through the quality of the people we place."*
